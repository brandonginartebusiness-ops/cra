# HB 837 — Florida Tort Reform (March 2023)

## Overview

Signed **March 24, 2023** — major civil litigation reform with significant downstream effects on insurance claims.

Sources: [Gen Re Analysis](https://www.genre.com/us/knowledge/publications/2024/march/floridas-tort-reform-revolution-en) · [Holland & Knight](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2023/03/florida-enacts-major-tort-reform-and-bad-faith-insurance-claim)

## Key provisions

### Modified comparative negligence
Florida moved from **pure to modified comparative negligence**. Recovery is now **barred** if the plaintiff is found to be **50% or more at fault**. Previously, a plaintiff could recover even if 99% at fault (with proportional reduction).

### Statute of limitations cut
Negligence statute of limitations: **4 years → 2 years**

### Attorney fees
Further eliminated one-way attorney fee provisions (building on [[03-Florida-Law/sb-2a-2022|SB 2-A]])

## Measured impacts

| Metric | Change |
|--------|--------|
| Insurance litigation filings, 2023→2024 | **-23% year-over-year** |
| Insurance litigation filings, H1 2025 vs H1 2024 | **-25%** |
| Florida nuclear verdict ranking | Fell from **2nd to 10th nationally** |
| Lawsuits filed in week before HB 837 took effect | **70,000+** (anticipatory rush) |

## Relevance to CRA

- Reduced litigation environment changes the leverage PA firms have in negotiation
- The 2-year negligence SOL is separate from the 1-year property insurance claim deadline ([[03-Florida-Law/sb-2a-2022|SB 2-A]])
- Understanding the litigation environment is important context for content about "bad faith" claims
